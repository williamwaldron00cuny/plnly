# Competitive Landscape — Round 2 (Affluent Households + College-Prep AI Literacy)

*Second pass, run after repositioning PLNLY toward affluent, time-poor households (not tech-anxious novices) plus AI-tool-literacy coaching for college-bound teens (not subject tutoring, not AI-safety/guardrails).*

## Bottom line

The niche holds up, and it holds up better than the first version. The adjacent categories — luxury concierge for affluent NYC families, human executive-assistant services, and AI-powered family-scheduling apps — are all established and funded, which means this audience is already primed to pay for "buy back my time." Nobody found is doing the specific thing PLNLY proposes: an actual person, in Brooklyn, who sets up and tunes AI tools around a specific household, then teaches the family to run it themselves. Same finding on the teen side — plenty of *content* exists about using AI tools well for college prep, but no one-on-one, in-person coaching service for it.

## Household AI-ops landscape

[**Ohai.ai**](http://Ohai.ai) — an AI assistant platform for family scheduling and coordination, founded January 2024 by Sheila Lirio Marcelo (founder of [Care.com](http://Care.com)). This is a software product, fully remote, not NYC-specific — but its existence is a useful signal: a well-known operator with a strong network bet real money on "AI assistant for household coordination" being a category families will pay for.

**Proxy** — a text-based AI assistant marketed specifically to parents for the "thinking side" of household management: narrowing decisions, remembering details, thinking ahead (e.g., flagging a preschool registration deadline before it's missed). Software, not a person, and not local.

**Sundays / AskSunday** — human executive-assistant services (Sundays specifically markets to "ambitious parents," staffed by a U.S.-based team of mostly parents, AI-enhanced). These are delegation services: you hand off a task, they do it. AskSunday runs a dedicated-assistant model with a free trial period before monthly billing kicks in.

**General luxury concierge (ELM, At The White Glove, etc.)** — broad "anything legal and ethical" concierge services for high-net-worth individuals and busy professionals: travel, errands, household vendors, events. Fidelity's wealth-management research notes that clients with $30M+ in investable assets rank concierge services as the single most important non-financial service they're offered — confirming this audience values exactly this kind of help, even if none of these specific firms touch AI setup.

**The gap, restated** — every option above is either delegation (a person or AI that does the task *for* you, ongoing dependency, recurring cost that doesn't go down) or a standalone software product (no human setup, no in-home tailoring, no teaching). PLNLY sits in neither category: it's a person who configures the tools *and* teaches the household to use them, so the family ends up more capable and less dependent — not less.

## Teen AI-literacy / college-prep landscape

There's real content here, and it's reassuring: the framing Billy described — "how to use NotebookLM, how to make sure your citations are right" — matches the emerging consensus on the *correct* way for students to use AI, not a workaround. Google's NotebookLM has dedicated student-facing features (source-grounded answers, citation-backed study guides, a distinct under-18 product experience) and is being pushed into classrooms via partnerships with Columbia Teachers College, ASU, and NYU Tisch. General advice articles (e.g., [kidsaitools.com](http://kidsaitools.com)'s college-prep roundup) draw the same ethical line Billy drew: "AI helps you organize your ideas, not generate ideas for you."

What's missing is delivery. All of this lives as scattered YouTube videos, blog posts, and generic platform features — nothing that looks like a person sitting down with a specific kid, on their specific laptop, and setting them up to use these tools correctly for their specific application season. That's the open lane.

One practical note for curriculum design later (not a blocker now): NotebookLM has a distinct, more limited product experience for users under 18, so the exact toolset/workflow taught may need to flex depending on the student's age and which Google Workspace/education access they have through their school.

## Pricing signal

Nothing here gives a clean "this is the number," but the signal is favorable. The Fidelity UHNW data point above, plus Sundays/AskSunday's positioning as an *affordable alternative* to a traditional full-time EA, suggests there's room between "free YouTube tutorial" and "$1,800+/month full EA retainer" for a setup-plus-teaching service priced well above the old tutoring-hour comps from the first scan. The earlier $180–250/month "light maintenance" retainer figure (from the first scan's personal-assistant research) is still a reasonable anchor for PLNLY's ongoing-management tier, with the initial setup/teaching engagement priced as its own project fee rather than folded into the monthly rate.

## Recommendation

The sharpest line from Billy's own framing — clients who "want to have an AI agent set up... yet still in control of their own itineraries" — is the differentiator. Lean into ownership and capability-building as the opposite of the delegation model (Sundays, AskSunday, traditional EAs) and the opposite of a black-box app (Ohai, Proxy). PLNLY doesn't run your life for you and doesn't hand you an app and walk away — it leaves you more capable than it found you, with the tools configured around how you actually live.

For the teen offering, the framing already matches where the field is heading (NotebookLM's own positioning, Google's education partnerships), which lowers the "is this even a real need" risk considerably. The work is in delivery — personal, in-person, specific to the kid — not in convincing anyone that AI-tool literacy for college applications matters.
