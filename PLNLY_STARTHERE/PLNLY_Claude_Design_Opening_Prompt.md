# PLNLY — Brand Identity Session: Opening Prompt for Claude Design

I'm starting a brand identity process for a new venture called PLNLY. I've uploaded a few reference documents — please read all of them before we begin, because they contain important context about who I am, my design taste, and the business we're building. Don't skip them or treat them as background noise; they are the brief.

Here's a plain-language summary of what we're building, so you have it up front.

## The brand

**PLNLY** is the written, stylized wordmark. **Plainly** is the spoken name. Both are in use simultaneously — "Plainly" always appears beneath the PLNLY wordmark as a small pronunciation hint, a designed reveal rather than an afterthought. That lockup is the core logo treatment and is already decided; we won't be reopening the name.

The business is a personal AI setup, teaching, and retainer service — based in Brooklyn, NY, eventually online. Two audiences: (1) affluent, time-constrained households who want AI working for their daily lives — scheduling, groceries, travel, household ops — but want to own the capability themselves, not outsource it; and (2) parents of college-bound teens who want their kids to develop real, responsible AI tool literacy before they get to campus. The through-line across both: every competitor in this space *delegates* — they do the AI work for clients. PLNLY hands over the keys. This is an ownership brand, not a concierge service, and that distinction should be felt in the visual identity.

**Umbrella brand promise:** "Modern life, minus the jargon."

## The naming architecture

PLNLY is the umbrella brand — the container. Individual service lines will eventually sit underneath it as sub-brands. The sub-brand naming convention isn't fully decided yet, but the system needs to be designed with that future in mind: the logo, color system, and typographic language should have enough flexibility that a sub-brand can share DNA with PLNLY without being identical to it. Think of it the way a hotel group has properties with their own names and feels that still clearly belong to the same family.

For now, the only live entity is the umbrella brand itself. Don't design it as if it will always be alone.

## What I need from this session

I want to go through the full brand identity process — not just a logo and a color swatch, but the whole thing, done properly. I don't have a design background, and I'm not entirely sure what I don't know to ask for. So here's what I'd like you to do:

**Walk me through the process.** Don't just generate options and wait. Explain what each stage of a real brand identity process involves — what decisions need to be made, in what order, and why. Help me discover the things I wouldn't know to ask for on my own: brand architecture, logo variations and use cases, color system logic (primary, secondary, neutral, functional), typographic hierarchy, spacing and grid basics, icon or illustration language, motion and voice as extensions of visual identity, and whatever else belongs in a complete system. Treat this like a collaborative studio process, not a one-shot generation.

**Then build it with me, iteratively.** For each stage: show me the direction, explain the choices you made and why they fit the brand brief, and give me a way to react and redirect before we move forward. I want to understand what I'm approving, not just pick from options blindly.

## Visual direction — a starting point, not a constraint

The uploaded About Me document has detailed taste references. Here's the short version to orient you:

Layered neutrals — greige, warm tones, soft blues, greens, greys — anchored by one confident pop of color used sparingly (coral, orange, warm yellow, or muted pink). Think Park Hyatt or Edition Miami: warm, considered, sleek without being cold. Aesop's packaging is the restraint reference — simple, uniform, a little mysterious, quietly high-end. Quality everyday-object energy (Aer, Bellroy, Shinola) rather than logo-screaming or trend-chasing.

Typography: design-forward and quiet. Not generic SaaS sans-serif-plus-gradient. Not twee or hand-lettered. The warmth in this brand should read as competence, not as dumbing-down.

**Hard avoids:** tech-bro blue-and-white SaaS palette, gradient mesh backgrounds, AI visual clichés (circuit patterns, neural-network line art, robot imagery), anything that reads as startup-generic or corporate-cold.

## Where this goes

Once we have a direction, I plan to carry the system into a one-page website, social presence, and eventually print materials (business cards, a client welcome packet). Design the system to scale — not as a single static image but as a living set of rules that can be applied consistently across surfaces.

When we have something strong, I'll export it for production use — likely via Canva for some surfaces, and eventually handed off to Claude Code or a developer for the website.

Before generating anything: tell me what information you still need from me to do this well, ask any clarifying questions, and then lay out the stages of the process we're about to go through so I know what to expect. Once I've confirmed I'm ready, we'll begin.
